# Core Competence (Prahalad & Hamel, 1990)

## Original Source

**Authors:** C.K. Prahalad & Gary Hamel
**Publication:** "The Core Competence of the Corporation", *Harvard Business Review*, May-June 1990, Vol. 68, No. 3, pp. 79-91
**Extended in:** *Competing for the Future* (book), HBS Press, 1994

## Canonical Protocol

### Definition

"Core competence is the collective learning in the organization, especially how to coordinate diverse production skills and integrate multiple streams of technologies."

Core competence is NOT about outspending rivals on R&D, shared costs between SBUs, or vertical integration. It is about the organization's ability to harmonize streams of technology, organize work, and deliver value.

### The Tree Metaphor (Exact Formulation)

> "The diversified corporation is a large tree. The trunk and major limbs are **core products**, the smaller branches are **business units**; the leaves, flowers, and fruit are **end products**. The root system that provides nourishment, sustenance, and stability is the **core competence**. You can miss the strength of competitors by looking only at their end products, in the same way you miss the strength of a tree if you look only at its leaves."

Three-level architecture:
1. **Core competence** (roots) — fundamental capabilities
2. **Core products** (trunk) — physical embodiments of competencies, components or subassemblies
3. **End products** (leaves/fruit) — what the customer buys

### The Three Tests (Exact Criteria)

A competence is "core" if and only if it passes ALL three tests:

| Test | Question | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| 1. **Market access** | Does it provide potential access to a wide variety of markets? | Honda's engine competence → cars, motorcycles, lawnmowers, generators |
| 2. **Customer benefit** | Does it make a significant contribution to the perceived customer benefits of the end product? | Honda's engines → customers perceive reliability, smooth performance |
| 3. **Difficult to imitate** | Is it difficult for competitors to imitate? Based on complex harmonization of individual technologies and production skills | Canon's combination of optics, imaging, microprocessor controls — not any single one, but the integration |

### Identification Protocol (from the original article)

Prahalad & Hamel prescribed the following approach:
1. Compile a list of 5-6 fundamental competencies (most companies will be unable to list more than 5-6)
2. Test each against the three criteria above
3. Map the competence to core products to end products — understand the architecture
4. Assess whether the company's strategic architecture cultivates these competencies or whether the SBU structure has imprisoned resources

### The SBU Prison Warning

The authors argue that the SBU (Strategic Business Unit) structure — dominant in Western corporations — systematically undermines core competence by:
- Fragmenting competencies across units
- Rewarding SBU-level P&L over cross-unit competence building
- Making competence "hostage" to whichever SBU currently uses it
- Allowing competencies to atrophy through underinvestment

### The NEC vs. GTE Comparison (Central Case)

In the early 1980s, GTE was larger and better positioned than NEC in information technology. By 1990:
- **NEC** — top 5 in telecom, semiconductors, and mainframes — built a "strategic architecture" around C&C (Computers & Communications) and systematically invested in core competencies
- **GTE** — reduced to a telephone operating company with lighting and defense — "could not agree on which competencies to base its strategy on"

## Validation Context

The article draws primarily on cases of Japanese companies (NEC, Honda, Canon, Casio) versus Western competitors (GTE, Chrysler, Kodak). The framework was embraced rapidly by corporate strategists in the 1990s, influencing restructuring at companies like Philips, Unilever, and others.

## Known Limitations

**The "Core Rigidities" Paradox (Leonard-Barton, 1992):**
Dorothy Leonard-Barton demonstrated that core capabilities have a "dark side" — they can become **core rigidities**. The same deep capabilities that provide competitive advantage become dysfunctional when the environment shifts. "Core rigidities may result from the institutionalization of knowledge and routines in a way that new approaches and information are not supported." Four dimensions: (1) employee skills, (2) technical systems, (3) managerial systems, (4) values/norms — the last is "an often overlooked but critical fourth dimension" that is hardest to change.

**The Capabilities Counter-argument (Stalk, Evans & Shulman, 1992):**
George Stalk et al. in "Competing on Capabilities" (*HBR*, March 1992) argued that competencies are too narrowly technical. Capabilities — which span the entire value chain and include business processes — matter more. Competence is about technological/production harmonization; capability is about the whole organization's ability to deliver value.

**Vagueness Criticism:**
Multiple researchers noted that the concept is "too vague for practical application" — it is difficult to operationally distinguish core from non-core competencies. The three tests leave significant room for subjective interpretation.

**Identification Problem:**
In practice, companies struggle to identify their core competencies reliably. What passes the "three tests" in one strategic context may fail in another. The framework offers weak guidance on how to build new competencies from scratch.

**Static Bias:**
The framework focuses on existing competencies. Dynamic capabilities theory (Teece, Pisano & Shuen, 1997) later addressed this gap — the ability to reconfigure competencies matters as much as having them.
