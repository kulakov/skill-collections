# Emergent Strategy (Mintzberg & Waters, 1985)

## Original Source

**Authors:** Henry Mintzberg & James A. Waters
**Publication:** "Of Strategies, Deliberate and Emergent", *Strategic Management Journal*, Vol. 6, No. 3, pp. 257-272, July-September 1985
**Prior foundation:** Mintzberg, "Patterns in Strategy Formation", *Management Science*, 1978

## Canonical Protocol

### Core Framework

Mintzberg & Waters define two poles of a continuum:
- **Deliberate strategy** — realized as intended
- **Emergent strategy** — patterns or consistencies realized despite, or in the absence of, intentions

Three conditions for a purely deliberate strategy:
1. Precise intentions articulated in concrete detail, leaving no room for interpretation
2. Shared by all actors in the organization (or accepted from leadership)
3. Realized exactly as intended, with no interference from external forces

A purely emergent strategy requires consistency in action without any hint of intention — a theoretical limit, not a practical reality.

### The Eight Types (Deliberate-to-Emergent Continuum)

| # | Type | Locus of Control | Deliberate/Emergent | Key Characteristics |
|---|------|-----------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Planned** | Central leadership | Most deliberate | Precise intentions formally articulated. Formal controls ensure articulated implementation. Formulation separated from implementation. Environment predictable or controllable. |
| 2 | **Entrepreneurial** | Single leader | Primarily deliberate | Personal vision of a single leader. Adaptable to new opportunities — vision provides direction, not specific details. Organization under personal control of the leader. |
| 3 | **Ideological** | Collective | Primarily deliberate | Shared vision held collectively with near-missionary zeal. Controlled through indoctrination and socialization. Organization proactive vis-a-vis environment. Difficult to change. |
| 4 | **Umbrella** | Partially delegated | Deliberately emergent | Leadership defines boundaries/targets but allows others to maneuver within them. "Deliberately emergent" — leadership controls the process indirectly. Common in complex, unpredictable environments. |
| 5 | **Process** | Delegated, process-controlled | Deliberately emergent | Leadership controls the process of strategy (who gets hired, what structures exist) but not the content. Strategy emerges within designed processes. |
| 6 | **Unconnected** | Subunit/individual | Emergent (for organization) | Strategy produced by an actor or subunit loosely coupled to the rest. Deliberate for the actor, but unconnected to (and emergent for) the organization. |
| 7 | **Consensus** | Collective convergence | Emergent | Actors converge on patterns through mutual adjustment, without central direction or shared prior intentions. Strategy grows out of collective action. |
| 8 | **Imposed** | Environment | Most emergent | Environment directly dictates patterns in actions. Organization has little choice. Least deliberate — the organization responds to external mandate. |

### How to Identify Emergent Patterns

Per the original paper: track realized strategy (what actually happened) and compare against intended strategy (what was planned). The gap reveals emergent elements. Track patterns in decisions over time across multiple domains. Pure forms are rare — real strategies combine deliberate and emergent elements.

## Validation Context

Mintzberg & Waters drew on longitudinal research at McGill University tracking strategies of multiple organizations including:
- Steinberg Inc. (Canadian supermarket chain, 1917-1974)
- Volkswagenwerk (1934-1974)
- U.S. government in Vietnam
- National Film Board of Canada

The framework emerged from 11 intensive research studies tracking strategy formation retrospectively over decades.

## Known Limitations

**As acknowledged by authors:**
- The eight types are "not any firm or exhaustive typology (although one may eventually emerge)" — presented as a working framework, not a definitive classification
- Neither purely deliberate nor purely emergent strategies exist in practice — both are theoretical limits
- A purely deliberate strategy "precludes learning" while a purely emergent one "precludes control"

**From critics:**
- **Retrospective rationalization problem** (Ansoff): emergent patterns are identified after the fact — are they genuinely emergent or just retrospectively rationalized?
- **Lacks prescriptive power**: tells you what types exist but not which to choose or how to manage the transition
- **Theoretical underpinning gap** (Foss, McCaffrey, Dorobat, 2022): the framework lacks a clear mechanism explaining how emergent strategy actually forms — rules and institutions as organizational frameworks are underspecified
- **"No strategy" problem**: if there is no pattern, there is no strategy by definition — this creates a definitional circularity where any consistent behavior becomes "strategy"
- **The Ansoff critique**: Mintzberg's approach can lead to "strategic drift" — organizations rationalizing reactive behavior as emergent strategy rather than making hard deliberate choices
