# Logical Incrementalism (Quinn)

## 1. Original Source

- **Author:** James Brian Quinn
- **Article:** "Strategic Change: Logical Incrementalism", Sloan Management Review, Fall 1978, pp. 7-21
- **Book:** "Strategies for Change: Logical Incrementalism", Homewood, IL: Dow Jones-Irwin, 1980
- **Also:** "Managing Strategic Change", Sloan Management Review, Summer 1980
- **Research base:** In-depth study of strategic change processes at **10 major corporations** including Chrysler, Exxon, General Mills, Pilkington, Pillsbury, and Xerox

---

## 2. Canonical Protocol

### The Central Finding

Quinn's research revealed that executives in large organizations do NOT follow the textbook model of:
1. Set objectives → 2. Analyze environment → 3. Generate alternatives → 4. Select strategy → 5. Implement

Instead, they **artfully blend formal analysis, behavioral techniques, and power politics** to bring about cohesive, step-by-step movement toward ends which initially are broadly conceived, but are then **constantly refined and reshaped** as new information appears.

### Three Positions on Strategy Formation — Quinn's Map

Quinn positioned his finding against two existing views:

| Approach | Proponent | Description | Quinn's verdict |
|----------|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Formal Planning** | Ansoff, Andrews | Rational-analytical, sequential, comprehensive | Bureaucratic, disconnected from real decisions |
| **Muddling Through** | Lindblom (1959) | Disjointed incrementalism, reactive, no guiding vision | Descriptive of process but unfair to executives |
| **Logical Incrementalism** | Quinn | Intentional, guided, incremental, politically aware | What actually happens in well-managed firms |

**Key distinction from Lindblom:** Quinn found that executives DO NOT "muddle" — they **direct** decision-making processes toward a long-term goal. The incrementalism is **logical** (purposeful, directed) not **disjointed** (reactive, fragmented).

**Key distinction from formal planning:** Strategy is not formulated-then-implemented as two sequential phases. Formulation and implementation are **fused** — strategy emerges through a continuous process of learning, testing, and commitment-building.

### The Process — Quinn's Observed Stages

#### Stage 1: Sensing Needs and General Concern
- A vaguely felt awareness of an issue or opportunity emerges
- Typically begins with unstructured sensing by top executives — conversations, data anomalies, competitive signals
- No formal problem definition yet

#### Stage 2: Broadcasting a General Idea (Without Details)
- The executive "floats" a general direction — without a detailed plan
- Purpose: elicit reactions pro and con, identify supporters and resisters, refine understanding
- The idea is deliberately vague to avoid premature commitment or opposition

#### Stage 3: Building Awareness and Amplifying Understanding
- Commissioning studies, creating task forces, hiring consultants — not primarily for their output, but to **educate the organization** and build shared understanding
- "Changing symbols" — new language, new metrics, new organizational signals that legitimize new viewpoints

#### Stage 4: Creating Credibility and Legitimizing New Viewpoints
- Early small wins that demonstrate the viability of the new direction
- Placing supporters in key positions
- Removing or reassigning opponents (but gradually, to avoid political disruption)

#### Stage 5: Tactical Shifts and Partial Solutions
- Implementing pieces of the strategy through discrete decisions — each defensible on its own merits
- "Systematic waiting" — deliberately delaying commitment until more information is available or until the political landscape shifts
- Building "pockets of commitment" throughout the organization

#### Stage 6: Broadening Support and Managing Coalitions
- Identifying zones of opposition and indifference
- Changing perceived risks — showing how the new direction reduces threats or creates opportunities for various stakeholders
- Building coalitions across organizational levels and functions

#### Stage 7: Crystallizing Focus and Formalizing Commitment
- Often triggered by a **crisis or opportunity** that makes the new direction seem urgent and inevitable
- At this point, the strategy that was emerging incrementally becomes "the official strategy"
- Formal plans, budgets, and structures are aligned — but they are **ratifying decisions already made**, not creating new ones

### Critical Process Characteristics

Quinn identified these recurring patterns across all 10 firms:

1. **Subsystem approach:** Major strategic changes are managed through **subsystems** (diversification subsystem, divestiture subsystem, technology subsystem, etc.) — each evolving on its own timeline with its own political dynamics

2. **No single comprehensive decision:** Strategy is not decided at one point in time. It crystallizes from a **stream of decisions** that gradually converge

3. **Lead times vary enormously:** Some subsystems require years of preparation (technology, culture change); others can move quickly (financial restructuring). The manager must juggle different time horizons simultaneously

4. **Political management is integral:** Building consensus, managing opposition, creating commitment — these are not "implementation problems" that follow strategy. They ARE strategy formation.

5. **Formal planning as one input:** Formal analysis and planning DO play a role — but as inputs to the incremental process, not as the process itself. Plans are "frameworks for thought," not blueprints for action.

### Quinn's 16 Process Activities

The complete list of management processes Quinn identified:

1. Sensing needs
2. Amplifying understanding
3. Building awareness
4. Creating credibility
5. Legitimizing viewpoints
6. Generating partial solutions
7. Broadening support
8. Identifying zones of opposition and indifference
9. Changing perceived risks
10. Structuring needed flexibilities
11. Putting forward trial concepts
12. Creating pockets of commitment
13. Eliminating undesired options
14. Crystallizing focus and consensus
15. Managing coalitions
16. Formalizing agreed-upon commitments

## 3. Validation Context

- **Research base:** 10 Fortune 500-class diversified corporations, studied through in-depth interviews with executives at multiple levels
- **Method:** Qualitative, longitudinal — Quinn traced strategic decisions over multi-year periods, not snapshots
- **Replication:** The findings were consistent across all 10 firms, across different industries (oil, food, glass, electronics, automotive)
- **The framework became one of the pillars of the "process school" of strategy** (alongside Mintzberg's emergent strategy concept)
- **Widely adopted** in Mintzberg, Ahlstrand & Lampel's "Strategy Safari" as one of the 10 schools of strategic management (the Learning School)

---

## 4. Known Limitations

### Structural weaknesses

1. **No guarantee of coherence:** The major weakness is that loosely linked incremental decisions **may not add up** to fulfillment of corporate purposes. The process can produce strategic drift — each decision locally rational but collectively directionless.

2. **Vulnerability to special interests:** Incrementalism reaches its limits when powerful factions exercise veto power, or when the organization faces complex investment decisions or crises requiring long-term integrated solutions.

3. **Slow for fast-moving environments:** The process Quinn described took **years** in each firm. In industries with rapid technological change or short competitive cycles, there may not be time for gradual consensus-building.

4. **Descriptive, not prescriptive:** Quinn documented what successful executives DO — he did not provide a clear method for executives who want to LEARN this approach. The skills required (political acuity, patience, timing) are tacit and hard to teach.

5. **Survivorship bias:** Quinn studied successful large firms. Incremental approaches in unsuccessful firms may look like indecision, drift, or bureaucratic inertia — but those firms weren't in the sample.

6. **Scale assumption:** The framework was developed in large, diversified corporations with hundreds of managers and complex political structures. Its applicability to startups, mid-size firms, or non-corporate settings is uncertain.

### Quinn's own caveat

Quinn explicitly acknowledged that logical incrementalism is not appropriate for all situations. He argued it works best when:
- The strategic situation is **complex and uncertain**
- Multiple powerful stakeholders must be aligned
- The organization is **large** and politically complex
- Time allows for gradual movement
