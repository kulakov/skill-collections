# Porter's Value Chain Analysis

## Original Source

**Author:** Michael E. Porter (Harvard Business School)
**Canonical work:** *Competitive Advantage: Creating and Sustaining Superior Performance* (1985), Free Press -- Chapter 2: "The Value Chain and Competitive Advantage"
**Related article:** Porter & Millar, "How Information Gives You Competitive Advantage," *Harvard Business Review*, July 1985

---

## Canonical Protocol

### Core Premise

Competitive advantage cannot be understood by looking at a firm as a whole. It stems from the many **discrete activities** a firm performs in designing, producing, marketing, delivering, and supporting its product. Each of these activities can contribute to a firm's relative cost position and create a basis for differentiation.

The value chain **disaggregates** a firm into its strategically relevant activities in order to understand the behavior of costs and the existing and potential sources of differentiation.

### The Value Chain Model

```
|--------------------------------------------------------------|
|                    FIRM INFRASTRUCTURE                         |
|--------------------------------------------------------------|
|                 HUMAN RESOURCE MANAGEMENT                      |
|--------------------------------------------------------------|
|                  TECHNOLOGY DEVELOPMENT                        |
|--------------------------------------------------------------|
|                      PROCUREMENT                               |
|--------------------------------------------------------------|
| Inbound   |           | Outbound  | Marketing |              |
| Logistics | Operations| Logistics | & Sales   |   Service    |
|--------------------------------------------------------------|
                                                        → MARGIN
```

### Primary Activities (5)

Activities directly involved in creating and delivering the product/service:

1. **Inbound Logistics** -- Receiving, storing, and disseminating inputs to the product. Includes material handling, warehousing, inventory control, vehicle scheduling, and returns to suppliers

2. **Operations** -- Transforming inputs into the final product form. Includes machining, packaging, assembly, equipment maintenance, testing, printing, and facility operations

3. **Outbound Logistics** -- Collecting, storing, and physically distributing the product to buyers. Includes finished goods warehousing, material handling, delivery vehicle operation, order processing, and scheduling

4. **Marketing and Sales** -- Providing a means by which buyers can purchase the product and inducing them to do so. Includes advertising, promotion, sales force, quoting, channel selection, channel relations, and pricing

5. **Service** -- Providing service to enhance or maintain the value of the product. Includes installation, repair, training, parts supply, and product adjustment

### Support Activities (4)

Activities that support the primary activities and each other:

1. **Firm Infrastructure** -- General management, planning, finance, accounting, legal, government affairs, quality management. Unlike other support activities, infrastructure typically supports the **entire chain**, not individual activities

2. **Human Resource Management** -- Recruiting, hiring, training, development, and compensation of all types of personnel. HRM affects competitive advantage through its role in determining skills and motivation of employees

3. **Technology Development** -- Every value activity embodies technology (know-how, procedures, process technology). Technology development consists of a range of activities from basic research and product design to media research, process equipment design, and servicing procedures

4. **Procurement** -- The function of purchasing inputs used in the firm's value chain. Purchased inputs include raw materials, supplies, and other consumable items as well as assets such as machinery, laboratory equipment, office equipment, and buildings. Procurement tends to be spread throughout the firm

### Key Concepts

#### Margin
Margin = Total Value Created - Collective Cost of Performing Value Activities. The difference between the total value (what buyers are willing to pay) and the cost of performing the value activities.

#### Linkages
**Critical to Porter's framework:** Value activities are not independent but **interdependent**. Linkages are relationships between the way one activity is performed and the cost or performance of another. Linkages often create trade-offs.

**Examples:**
- Higher quality inbound logistics (more careful inspection) can reduce operations costs (less rework)
- More expensive product design and better materials can reduce service costs
- A more expensive sales force (better trained) can reduce post-sale service needs

Competitive advantage frequently derives from linkages among activities just as it does from the individual activities themselves. Linkages can lead to competitive advantage in two ways: **optimization** and **coordination**.

#### The Value System
The firm's value chain is embedded in a larger stream of activities -- the **value system**. This includes:
- Supplier value chains (create and deliver purchased inputs)
- Channel value chains (distribution and delivery to buyer)
- Buyer value chains (where the firm's product becomes an input)

Understanding the entire value system is necessary for a complete picture of competitive advantage.

### Porter's Analytical Process (Steps)

**For Cost Advantage:**
1. **Identify the firm's value chain** -- disaggregate into specific activities
2. **Assign costs and assets** to each value activity
3. **Identify the cost drivers** of each activity (scale, learning, capacity utilization, linkages, interrelationships, integration, timing, discretionary policies, location, institutional factors)
4. **Identify competitors' value chains** and determine their relative costs
5. **Develop a strategy** to lower cost position through controlling cost drivers or reconfiguring the value chain

**For Differentiation Advantage:**
1. **Identify the buyer's value chain** -- determine what the buyer really values
2. **Identify the firm's value chain** -- disaggregate into specific activities
3. **Identify differentiation variables** for each activity
4. **Identify linkages** between the firm's value chain and the buyer's chain that create differentiation opportunity
5. **Assess sustainability** of the differentiation sources (barriers to imitation, multiple sources of differentiation, cost of differentiation relative to price premium)

## Validation Context

- Porter developed the framework through analysis of numerous industries in *Competitive Advantage*, with detailed examples from **copiers (Xerox), chocolate (Hershey/Mars), airlines, steel, and consumer electronics**
- The value chain became the standard framework for **internal analysis** in strategic management, complementing Five Forces (external analysis) and generic strategies (positioning)
- Widely adopted in management consulting for **cost structure analysis, process reengineering, and outsourcing decisions**
- Applied extensively in **supply chain management** to analyze the full value system
- Porter & Millar (1985) extended it specifically to analyze how information technology transforms value activities

## Known Limitations

### Acknowledged by Porter (Implicitly)

- The framework is designed for **manufacturing and traditional service firms** with clear input-transformation-output logic. Porter's own examples are predominantly manufacturing-oriented

### From Critics

1. **Manufacturing bias:** The five primary activities assume a physical product flowing through a linear process. Service firms, software companies, and knowledge-intensive businesses find the categories awkward or inapplicable. Several adaptations have been proposed (e.g., Stabell & Fjeldstad's "value shop" and "value network" models, 1998)

2. **Firm-centric in a networked world:** The value chain focuses on a single firm. In modern ecosystems, platforms, and network-based businesses, value creation is distributed across multiple firms, and the boundaries of "the firm" are fluid. The value system concept partially addresses this but was not fully developed

3. **Static decomposition:** The value chain provides a snapshot but does not account for how activities and their importance change over time. Digital transformation can fundamentally reconfigure which activities matter

4. **Difficult to operationalize cost allocation:** Assigning costs and assets to individual activities is theoretically clean but practically challenging. Shared resources, overhead allocation, and transfer pricing make precise activity costing difficult

5. **Ignores intangible assets:** The framework struggles to capture value creation from brand, intellectual property, organizational culture, and tacit knowledge -- assets that do not neatly map to specific activities

6. **Linear assumption challenged:** Modern business models (platform businesses, freemium models, data-driven services) create value through network effects and feedback loops rather than linear value addition. The sequential chain metaphor breaks down

7. **Complementary, not standalone:** Porter himself designed the value chain to work WITH Five Forces and generic strategies. Used in isolation, it risks becoming a mere activity inventory without strategic direction
