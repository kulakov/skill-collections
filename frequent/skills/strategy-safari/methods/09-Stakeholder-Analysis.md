# Stakeholder Analysis (Freeman, 1984)

## Original Source

**Author:** R. Edward Freeman
**Publication:** *Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach*, Pitman Publishing, Boston, 1984 (reprinted by Cambridge University Press, 2010)
**Key chapter:** Chapter 2, "Stakeholder Management: Framework and Philosophy" (later republished as a standalone piece)

**Intellectual lineage:** Freeman built on Igor Ansoff (1965, who first used "stakeholders"), Stanford Research Institute (SRI) work from the 1960s, and the process work of Ian Mitroff, Richard Mason, and James Emshoff.

## Canonical Protocol

### The Foundational Definition

"A stakeholder in an organization is any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the achievement of the organization's objectives." (Freeman, 1984, p. 46)

### The Stakeholder Map

Freeman's canonical **hub-and-spoke diagram** places the firm at the center with spokes radiating to each stakeholder group. The original "generic stakeholder map" includes:

Owners, Employees, Customers, Suppliers, Competitors, Government, Media, Local Community, Financial Community, Activist Groups, Trade Associations, Political Groups

Freeman emphasized this is a starting point — each firm must construct its OWN stakeholder map through historical analysis of its specific environment.

### The Three Levels of Analysis

Freeman's framework operates at three levels, explicitly paralleling Graham Allison's (1971) three levels of organizational analysis:

**Level 1: Rational Level — "Understanding stakeholders and stakes"**
- Who are the stakeholders?
- What are their perceived stakes?
- What opportunities and challenges do stakeholders present?
- What responsibilities does the firm have toward each stakeholder?
- Produce: a stakeholder map with identified stakes for each group

**Level 2: Process Level — "Managing stakeholder relationships"**
- Do the firm's current processes (strategic planning, environmental scanning, etc.) take stakeholder concerns into account?
- Does the organization have processes that "fit" the rational stakeholder map?
- Are Standard Operating Procedures aligned with stakeholder realities?
- Key test: is there a systematic way stakeholder concerns get surfaced and addressed?

**Level 3: Transactional Level — "The actual bargains"**
- What actual transactions or bargains occur between the firm and its stakeholders?
- Do these transactions "fit" with the stakeholder map and the organizational processes?
- Is the firm interacting with stakeholders in ways consistent with stated strategy?

**The "Fit" Requirement:** The three levels must be consistent. Processes must fit the rational map. Transactions must fit the processes. Inconsistency = strategic vulnerability.

### The Stakeholder Audit

Freeman prescribed a systematic audit process:
1. Map all stakeholders (Level 1)
2. Assess each stakeholder's potential to **cooperate** with the firm
3. Assess each stakeholder's potential to **threaten** the firm
4. Classify stakeholders based on these two dimensions

### Generic Stakeholder Strategies

Based on the cooperative potential / threat potential matrix:

| | High Cooperative Potential | Low Cooperative Potential |
|---|---|---|
| **High Threat** | **Mixed Blessing** — Collaborate to align interests | **Non-supportive** — Defend against |
| **Low Threat** | **Supportive** — Involve and engage | **Marginal** — Monitor, minimal effort |

Freeman prescribed different engagement strategies for each category:
- **Supportive stakeholders**: involve directly in decision-making
- **Non-supportive stakeholders**: develop defensive strategies
- **Mixed blessing stakeholders**: collaborative strategy to increase cooperative potential while managing threat
- **Marginal stakeholders**: minimal resource allocation, monitoring only

## Validation Context

Freeman developed the framework during a period of increasing environmental turbulence for corporations in the 1970s-80s (oil crises, consumerism, environmentalism, deregulation). The framework was validated through:
- Case studies of firms navigating stakeholder conflicts
- Application at Wharton School's Applied Research Center
- Subsequent adoption across strategic management, business ethics, and corporate governance fields
- The book has been cited over 75,000 times (per Google Scholar), making it one of the most cited works in management

## Known Limitations

**The Stakeholder Paradox (Goodpaster, 1991):**
Kenneth Goodpaster identified a fundamental paradox: "It seems essential, yet in some ways illegitimate, to orient corporate decisions by ethical values that go beyond strategic stakeholder considerations to multi-fiduciary ones." If managers have fiduciary duties to shareholders, expanding those duties to all stakeholders creates irreconcilable obligations. Goodpaster's resolution: managers have duties to all stakeholders, but fiduciary duty only to shareholders.

**The "Too Broad" Problem:**
Freeman's definition ("any group or individual who can affect or is affected by") is so broad that virtually anyone — including competitors, the media, even terrorists — qualifies as a stakeholder. Mitchell, Agle & Wood (1997) identified 27 different stakeholder definitions in the literature and proposed urgency/legitimacy/power as filters. The question "who counts?" remains contested.

**Prioritization Problem:**
The original framework provides weak guidance on how to prioritize among competing stakeholder claims. When stakeholders' interests genuinely conflict (e.g., employees vs. shareholders, community vs. customers), the framework does not prescribe resolution mechanisms.

**Power-Interest Matrix Attribution:**
Note: The well-known **Power-Interest Matrix** (sometimes called the Mendelow Matrix) is commonly attributed to Freeman but was actually developed by Aubrey Mendelow (1991) and later by Eden & Ackermann. Freeman's original 1984 framework uses cooperative potential / threat potential, not power / interest as axes.

**Normative vs. Instrumental Confusion:**
Donaldson & Preston (1995) noted that stakeholder theory conflates three distinct uses: descriptive (what firms do), instrumental (what leads to better performance), and normative (what firms should do). Freeman's 1984 work mixes all three without clearly distinguishing them.
