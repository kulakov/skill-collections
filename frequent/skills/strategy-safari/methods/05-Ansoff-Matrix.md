# Ansoff's Product-Market Matrix

## Original Source

**Author:** H. Igor Ansoff (1918-2002), Russian-American mathematician; regarded as "the father of strategic management"
**First publication:** "Strategies for Diversification," *Harvard Business Review*, Vol. 35, No. 5, September-October 1957, pp. 113-124
**Extended treatment:** *Corporate Strategy: An Analytic Approach to Business Policy for Growth and Expansion* (1965), McGraw-Hill

### Key Definition from the 1957 Paper

Ansoff defined **product-market strategy** as "a joint statement of a product line and the corresponding set of missions which the products are designed to fulfill." The word "mission" is central -- Ansoff thought in terms of the job the product does (prefiguring Jobs-to-be-Done thinking by decades).

## Canonical Protocol

### The Growth Vector Matrix (Ansoff's Original Name)

|                     | **Present Products** | **New Products**      |
|---------------------|----------------------|-----------------------|
| **Present Markets** | Market Penetration   | Product Development   |
| **New Markets**     | Market Development   | Diversification       |

### The Four Strategies (Ansoff's Original Definitions)

#### 1. Market Penetration

**Definition:** Growth through increasing the share of existing products in existing markets.

**Ansoff's methods:**
- Increase frequency of use by existing customers
- Increase quantity purchased per occasion
- Find new applications for current users
- Attract competitors' customers through differentiation, pricing, or marketing

**Risk level:** LOWEST. The firm operates with familiar products and familiar markets. All existing organizational knowledge, skills, and relationships remain relevant.

#### 2. Market Development

**Definition:** Growth through finding new markets (geographic, demographic, or new market segments) for existing products.

**Ansoff's methods:**
- Geographic expansion (new regions, international markets)
- New customer segments (different demographics, industries, or use cases)
- New distribution channels
- Adapting pricing or positioning for different market contexts

**Risk level:** MODERATE. The product is known, but the market dynamics, customer needs, competitive landscape, and distribution requirements are unfamiliar.

#### 3. Product Development

**Definition:** Growth through developing new products for existing markets.

**Ansoff's methods:**
- New product features or models
- Quality variations (higher or lower)
- Technologically improved products
- Entirely new products serving the same customer need differently

**Risk level:** MODERATE (similar to market development). The market is known, but the product development, production, and technical capabilities required are new.

#### 4. Diversification

**Definition:** Growth through new products in new markets. This strategy stands fundamentally apart from the other three.

**Ansoff's critical distinction (1957):** "The first three strategies are usually pursued with the same technical, financial, and merchandising resources used for the original product line, whereas diversification usually requires a company to acquire new skills, new techniques and new facilities."

**Ansoff's sub-types of diversification:**

- **Horizontal diversification:** New products that are technologically unrelated to the existing product line but appeal to the existing customer base
- **Vertical diversification (integration):** Moving backward (toward suppliers) or forward (toward distribution/customers) in the value chain
- **Concentric diversification:** New products that have technological or marketing synergies with existing product lines, even though they appeal to a new customer group
- **Conglomerate diversification:** New products in new markets with no relationship to the firm's current technology, products, or markets

**Risk level:** HIGHEST. Both the product and the market are unfamiliar. The firm operates outside its existing competence base. Conglomerate diversification carries the highest risk of all four sub-types.

### Ansoff's Risk Logic

Risk increases as the firm moves away from its existing competence base:

```
Market Penetration  →  LOWEST RISK
         ↓
Market Development  →  MODERATE RISK
Product Development →  MODERATE RISK
         ↓
Diversification     →  HIGHEST RISK
    - Concentric    →  High
    - Horizontal    →  Higher
    - Conglomerate  →  Highest
```

Ansoff's core insight: **the further a firm moves from known products and known markets, the greater the uncertainty and the lower the probability of success.** This is not a reason to avoid diversification, but a reason to assess it rigorously and to prefer related diversification over unrelated when possible.

### Ansoff's Analytical Process (from Corporate Strategy, 1965)

1. **Define the current product-market scope** -- what products does the firm offer, and to which markets?
2. **Identify the growth vector** -- in which direction(s) should the firm expand?
3. **Assess competitive advantage** -- what gives (or would give) the firm an edge in each cell?
4. **Evaluate synergy** -- to what extent can the firm leverage existing resources, skills, and relationships for the growth strategy? (Ansoff was the first to use "synergy" as a strategic concept)
5. **Make the diversification vs. expansion decision** -- diversify only when existing product-market combinations are exhausted or when compelling synergies exist

### Synergy (Ansoff's Original Framework)

Ansoff identified four types of synergy:
1. **Sales synergy** -- shared distribution channels, sales administration, warehousing, advertising
2. **Operating synergy** -- shared facilities, personnel, learning curves, bulk purchasing
3. **Investment synergy** -- shared plant, machinery, tooling, raw materials inventory, technology transfer
4. **Management synergy** -- transferable management skills and problem-solving approaches

The "2+2=5 effect" -- Ansoff's phrase for the combined return from shared resources exceeding the sum of parts.

## Validation Context

- Ansoff developed the framework through analysis of **US industrial corporations in the 1950s-60s**, particularly defense and technology companies transitioning to civilian markets post-WWII
- The 1957 HBR paper drew on examples from **diversified industrial conglomerates** of the era
- *Corporate Strategy* (1965) became the foundational text for **corporate planning departments** in large firms
- The matrix was widely adopted by **GE, Westinghouse, and other diversified corporations** during the 1960s-70s conglomerate era
- Ansoff later founded the first dedicated strategic management department at Vanderbilt University and continued developing the framework through multiple editions

## Known Limitations

### Acknowledged by Ansoff (Partially)

- Ansoff recognized that diversification is not inherently desirable and should only be pursued when compelling synergies or market exhaustion justify the risk
- In later work (1990), Ansoff emphasized that the framework requires supplementation with environmental turbulence analysis

### From Critics

1. **Oversimplification / Binary categories:** Markets and products exist on a continuum, not in binary "existing vs. new" states. A product extension is not the same as an entirely new product. A new geographic market is not the same as an entirely new customer segment. The 2x2 structure flattens these differences

2. **Ignores competition:** The matrix focuses entirely on the firm's own growth direction. It does not account for competitors' strategies, responses, or the competitive dynamics of target markets. A market development strategy can fail not because of the firm's capabilities but because incumbents in the new market respond aggressively

3. **Inadequate risk assessment:** The risk gradient (penetration < development < diversification) is directionally correct but overly simplistic. Actual risk depends on dozens of factors the matrix does not capture: competitive intensity, regulatory barriers, capital requirements, cultural distance, technological complexity

4. **No timing or sequencing:** The matrix treats strategies as discrete choices at a point in time. It provides no guidance on sequencing (e.g., should you develop the product first, then enter new markets?), timing, or how to manage a portfolio of growth strategies simultaneously

5. **No external environment:** The matrix has no mechanism for incorporating macroeconomic trends, technological disruption, regulatory changes, or other environmental factors that can dramatically alter the viability of each strategy

6. **Growth bias:** The entire framework assumes growth is the objective. It provides no guidance for firms that should be defending, retrenching, divesting, or maintaining their current position. Growth is treated as inherently desirable

7. **Synergy as aspiration vs. reality:** Ansoff's synergy concept assumes firms can reliably capture the "2+2=5" effect. Decades of M&A research show that anticipated synergies are systematically overestimated and frequently fail to materialize (Sirower, 1997, "The Synergy Trap")
