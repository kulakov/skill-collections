# Porter's Five Forces

## Original Source

**Author:** Michael E. Porter (Harvard Business School)
**First publication:** "How Competitive Forces Shape Strategy," *Harvard Business Review*, March-April 1979
**Canonical work:** *Competitive Strategy: Techniques for Analyzing Industries and Competitors* (1980), Free Press, New York -- Chapter 1: "The Structural Analysis of Industries"
**Updated treatment:** "The Five Competitive Forces That Shape Strategy," *Harvard Business Review*, January 2008 (Porter's own 30-year revision)

**Intellectual foundation:** Structure-Conduct-Performance (SCP) paradigm from industrial organization economics (Joe Bain, 1950s-60s). Porter translated IO economics into a managerial framework.

---

## Canonical Protocol

### Core Premise

The essence of strategy formulation is **coping with competition**. But managers define competition too narrowly -- as if it occurred only among direct rivals. Competition for profits extends beyond established industry rivals to include four other forces. The collective strength of these five forces determines the ability of firms in an industry to earn, on average, rates of return on investment in excess of the cost of capital.

### The Five Forces (Porter's Original Definitions)

#### 1. Threat of New Entrants

New entrants bring new capacity, the desire to gain market share, and often substantial resources. The seriousness of the threat depends on **barriers to entry** and the **reaction from existing competitors** that the entrant can expect.

**Porter's 7 major sources of barriers to entry:**
1. **Supply-side economies of scale** -- spreading fixed costs over larger volume, forcing entrants to start large or accept cost disadvantage
2. **Demand-side benefits of scale (network effects)** -- buyers' willingness to purchase increases with the number of existing buyers
3. **Customer switching costs** -- one-time costs buyers face when changing suppliers
4. **Capital requirements** -- need to invest large financial resources to compete
5. **Incumbency advantages independent of size** -- cost or quality advantages from experience curve, proprietary technology, access to raw materials, favorable locations, established brand identities, patents
6. **Unequal access to distribution channels** -- limited shelf space, established relationships
7. **Government policy** -- licensing, regulation, subsidies to incumbents

#### 2. Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Suppliers can exert bargaining power by threatening to raise prices or reduce quality. Powerful suppliers can squeeze profitability out of an industry.

**A supplier group is powerful if:**
- It is more concentrated than the industry it sells to
- It does not depend heavily on the industry for its revenues
- Industry participants face switching costs in changing suppliers
- Suppliers offer products that are differentiated
- There is no substitute for what the supplier provides
- The supplier group can credibly threaten to integrate forward

#### 3. Bargaining Power of Buyers

Buyers compete with the industry by forcing down prices, bargaining for higher quality or more services, and playing competitors against each other.

**A buyer group is powerful if:**
- There are few buyers, or each purchases in volumes that are large relative to the size of a single vendor
- The products are standard or undifferentiated
- Buyers face few switching costs
- Buyers can credibly threaten to integrate backward
- The industry's product is unimportant to the quality of buyers' products or services
- The buyer has full information about demand, actual market prices, and supplier costs

#### 4. Threat of Substitute Products or Services

A substitute performs the same or a similar function as an industry's product by a different means. Substitutes limit the potential returns of an industry by placing a ceiling on the prices firms can charge.

**Substitutes are most dangerous when:**
- They offer an attractive price-performance trade-off to the industry's product
- The buyer's cost of switching to the substitute is low
- Substitutes come from industries earning high profits (and can afford to invest in displacing the current product)

#### 5. Rivalry Among Existing Competitors

Rivalry takes many forms: price discounting, new product introductions, advertising campaigns, service improvements. High rivalry limits the profitability of an industry.

**Rivalry is most intense when:**
- Competitors are numerous or roughly equal in size and power
- Industry growth is slow
- Exit barriers are high
- Rivals are highly committed to the business and have aspirations for leadership
- Firms cannot read each other's signals well

### The Analytical Process (Porter's Prescribed Steps)

1. **Define the relevant industry** -- identify the products, geographic scope, and the appropriate level of analysis (too broad = meaningless; too narrow = misses important forces)
2. **Identify the participants and segment them** into buyers, suppliers, competitors, substitutes, and potential entrants
3. **Assess the underlying drivers** of each force using the specific determinant checklists above
4. **Determine the overall industry structure** -- which forces are strongest and why
5. **Analyze recent and likely future changes** in each force
6. **Identify aspects of industry structure** that the firm's strategy might influence

### Key Insight: Industry Structure Determines Average Profitability

The five forces determine how the economic value created by an industry is apportioned -- how much is retained by firms vs. bargained away by buyers, suppliers, substitutes, entrants, or dissipated through rivalry.

## Validation Context

- Porter drew on extensive industry studies including **airlines, brewing, soft drinks, pharmaceuticals, trucking, and consumer electronics**
- The 1980 book contains detailed industry analysis examples throughout
- The framework became the standard tool for industry analysis in MBA programs worldwide within a decade of publication
- Porter's 2008 HBR update explicitly revisited the framework with examples from **internet industries, airlines (post-deregulation), and emerging market industries**
- Widely applied in consulting (McKinsey, BCG, Bain all incorporate five forces analysis into industry assessment)

## Known Limitations

### Acknowledged by Porter

- Porter himself (2008) clarified common misuses: the framework is about **industry structure**, not about listing things that are good or bad. The goal is to explain WHY an industry is more or less profitable, not to generate a strategy directly
- Porter acknowledged the difficulty of defining industry boundaries correctly

### From Critics

1. **Static model in dynamic industries:** The framework assumes relatively stable industry structures. In fast-moving technology markets, industry boundaries shift rapidly and the analysis becomes outdated (Downes, 1997; D'Aveni's "hypercompetition," 1994)

2. **Ignores complementors:** Brandenburger & Nalebuff (1996, *Co-opetition*) argued Porter's framework is purely adversarial and misses the critical role of **complementors** -- firms whose products increase the value of yours (e.g., app developers for a platform). They proposed this as a "sixth force"

3. **Adversarial assumption:** The model treats all relationships as zero-sum competition. Coyne & Subramaniam (1996) argued it ignores collusion, alliances, and cooperative value creation

4. **Platform and ecosystem blindness:** In platform businesses (Uber, Airbnb, Amazon Marketplace), boundaries between buyer/supplier/competitor blur. The same entity can occupy multiple roles simultaneously. Control over the ecosystem -- not the product -- becomes the primary source of market power

5. **Government as background, not force:** Many practitioners argue government regulation should be treated as a force in its own right, not merely as a modifier of other forces (especially in regulated industries, emerging markets)

6. **Overfocus on existing industry:** Five Forces analyzes an existing industry but provides poor guidance for firms creating entirely new markets (cf. Kim & Mauborgne's Blue Ocean Strategy, 2005)

7. **No guidance on firm-level action:** The framework explains industry profitability but does not prescribe what a specific firm should do. Porter addressed this separately with generic strategies and the value chain
