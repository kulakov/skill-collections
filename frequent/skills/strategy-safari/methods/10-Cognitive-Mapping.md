## Canonical Protocol

### Huff's Taxonomy: Five Types of Cognitive Maps

Anne Huff (1990) classified cognitive maps into five types, progressing from surface-level to deep structure:

| # | Type | What It Captures | Method |
|---|------|-----------------|--------|
| 1 | **Attention/Association Maps** | What concepts/topics a strategist pays attention to, and how they associate them | Content analysis of speeches, documents, media mentions |
| 2 | **Categorization Maps** | How a strategist organizes concepts into categories and taxonomies — what goes with what | Repertory grid, card sorting, cluster analysis |
| 3 | **Causal/Influence Maps** | How a strategist understands cause-and-effect — what leads to what | Causal mapping, interview-based elicitation |
| 4 | **Argument Maps** | The structure of reasoning — premises, conclusions, warrants, rebuttals | Argument analysis (Toulmin model) |
| 5 | **Schema/Frame Maps** | Deep cognitive structures — perceptual codes, frames, mental models that shape interpretation | Narrative semiotics, frame analysis |

Huff noted that most strategy research uses Type 3 (causal maps), but Types 1, 2, 4, and 5 offer distinct and underutilized insights.

### Eden's SODA Method: The Operational Process

SODA (Strategic Options Development and Analysis) was developed by Colin Eden at the University of Bath. It is a problem-structuring method, not a decision-making method — it helps groups define what the problem IS before trying to solve it.

**Theoretical Foundation: Personal Construct Theory (Kelly, 1955)**

Eden's key differentiator: concepts are **bipolar constructs** — each concept has two poles representing a range between two extremes. Example: not just "market share" but "growing market share ... declining market share." The contrast pole clarifies meaning.

**The SODA Process:**

**Phase 1: Individual Cognitive Mapping (Interview)**

1. **Conduct semi-structured interview** with each participant (typically 60-90 minutes)
2. **Capture concepts as short phrases** (8-12 words max) — write down what the person says, in their own language
3. **Make each concept bipolar**: write the preferred pole first, then "..." and the contrast pole. Example: "increase customer retention ... lose customers to competitors"
4. **Link concepts with directional arrows** indicating causality: A may lead to B (means-ends relationship)
5. **Build the map hierarchically**: Goals/outcomes at the top, options/actions at the bottom, strategic issues in the middle
   - **Head** of map = goals (superordinate concepts — desired ends)
   - **Tail** of map = options (subordinate concepts — potential actions)
   - Middle = strategic issues and assertions

**Map Notation Rules:**
- Arrow from A to B means "A may lead to B" (causality/influence)
- A **negative sign** on an arrow means the contrast pole of concept A leads to the first pole of concept B
- Concepts with many arrows going OUT = potent options
- Concepts with many arrows coming IN = busy goals or central issues

**Phase 2: Map Analysis**

Analyze the individual maps for:
- **Heads** (concepts with no outgoing arrows) = goals
- **Tails** (concepts with no incoming arrows) = options
- **Clusters** (dense interconnected groups of concepts) = strategic themes
- **Central concepts** (high connectivity) = key issues
- **Feedback loops** (circular causality) = systemic dynamics
- **Bridges** (concepts connecting clusters) = strategic leverage points

**Phase 3: Merging and Group Negotiation**

1. **Merge individual maps** into a composite group map
2. **Present the merged map** to the group using a facilitated workshop
3. **Negotiate meaning**: participants discuss where their maps agree and disagree
4. **Identify shared strategic issues**: clusters that appear across multiple individual maps
5. **Develop action portfolios**: options that address multiple goals simultaneously
6. **Prioritize**: the group negotiates which options to pursue

**Two Facilitators Required:**
- One attends to **content** (the map itself, what is being said)
- One attends to **process** (group dynamics, participation balance, time management)

**Software Support:** Eden and Ackermann developed Decision Explorer (originally COPE) as dedicated software for building, analyzing, and displaying cognitive maps.

## Validation Context

- Eden's original development at the University of Bath, applied to organizational consultancy
- Extensively used in UK public sector strategy development
- Applied in Shell's internal strategy processes (complementing scenario planning)
- Ackermann & Eden documented over 100 SODA interventions across organizations
- The 2020 survey (Georgiou et al.) documented SODA applications from 1989-2018 across diverse sectors

## Known Limitations

**As acknowledged by practitioners:**
- **Individual subjectivity**: maps capture one person's constructs — "the reality which is unique to him/her." Aggregating individual maps into a group map is conceptually problematic because "even if the constructs being captured appear to say the same thing, the map will not reflect a single cognition but rather fragments of a number of different cognitions"
- **Facilitator influence**: the facilitator's probing questions, emphasis, and interpretation inevitably shape the map — the map is always a co-construction, never a pure extraction
- **Static snapshot**: maps capture thinking at a point in time — they do not track how cognition evolves, which is often more strategically relevant
- **Oversimplification**: reducing complex strategic reasoning to nodes and arrows necessarily loses nuance, context, and the tacit knowledge that resists verbalization
- **Scale problems**: maps with 100+ concepts become unwieldy and hard to analyze without software; the sheer complexity can paralyze rather than clarify
- **Lacks formal constitutive rules** (acknowledged by researchers): SODA "needs a set of constitutive rules, and the task of producing these lies on SODA's creators, Eden and Ackermann" — the method remains more art than science
- **Values dependence**: "the direction of the arrow will depend on the value system of the individual (or group) being mapped" — different value systems produce different maps from the same facts, and the method provides no way to adjudicate between them

**From critics:**
- Hodgkinson et al. (2004) showed that different elicitation procedures (interview vs. self-report vs. document-based) produce significantly different causal maps for the same strategic situation — the method is not robust to procedural variation
- The link between cognitive maps and actual strategic behavior is empirically weak — managers do not necessarily act according to their articulated constructs
