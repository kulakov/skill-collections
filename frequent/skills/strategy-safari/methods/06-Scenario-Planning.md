# Scenario Planning (Pierre Wack / Shell Method, 1985; Peter Schwartz / GBN, 1991)

- Peter Schwartz, *The Art of the Long View: Planning for the Future in an Uncertain World*, Doubleday, 1991

## Canonical Protocol

### The Wack/Shell Method (Original)

Pierre Wack drew a fundamental distinction between two generations of scenarios:

**First-generation scenarios ("Learning Scenarios"):**
- Combine obvious uncertainties into alternative futures
- Provide contextual framing
- Wack found these insufficient: "Simply combining obvious uncertainties did not help much with decision making. That exercise brought us only to a set of obvious, simplistic, and conflicting strategic solutions."

**Second-generation scenarios ("Decision Scenarios"):**
- Tailored to the mental models of specific decision-makers
- Designed to challenge and transform the decision-makers' "microcosm" (their internal mental map of reality)
- "Unless we influenced the mental image, the picture of reality held by critical decision makers, our scenarios would be like water on a stone."

**The Core Insight — Reperceiving:**
Wack's key contribution was understanding that the purpose of scenarios is NOT prediction. The purpose is to change the minds of decision-makers. He called this "the gentle art of reperceiving" — a process involving:
1. Study of the business environment (through scenarios)
2. Rigorous examination of one's own intent
3. Analysis of competitive advantage
4. Assessment of strategic options

**Predetermined Elements vs. Uncertainties:**
Wack identified that some outcomes are virtually certain (predetermined elements — e.g., demographic shifts already in motion) while others are genuinely uncertain. Good scenarios separate these and build narratives around the uncertain elements while incorporating the predetermined ones as constants.

### The Schwartz/GBN Eight-Step Process (Codified Protocol)

Peter Schwartz formalized the Shell approach into eight explicit steps:

**Step 1: Identify the Focal Issue or Decision**
Start specific: "What decision is keeping you awake at night?" Begin with the specific decision, then build outward toward the environment. NOT "what will the world look like" but "what do we need to decide, and what do we need to know about the future to decide well?"

**Step 2: Key Forces in the Local Environment**
List the key factors influencing success or failure of that decision: customers, suppliers, competitors, regulatory changes, technology shifts in the immediate business environment.

**Step 3: Driving Forces (Macro-Environment)**
Identify the macro-environmental forces that drive the key local factors: Social, Technological, Economic, Environmental, Political (STEEP). Research deeply — this is the research-intensive step.

**Step 4: Rank by Importance and Uncertainty**
Create a 2x2 plot: Importance (for the focal decision) vs. Uncertainty (how unpredictable). The critical inputs are factors that are BOTH highly important AND highly uncertain. Predetermined elements (high importance, low uncertainty) appear in ALL scenarios.

**Step 5: Select Scenario Logics**
Identify the 2-3 factors that are most important and most uncertain. These become the axes of the scenario matrix. Typically produces 2-4 scenarios. The goal: scenarios whose differences make a difference to decision-makers. Avoid: more than 4 scenarios (cognitive overload), scenarios that differ only on predetermined elements.

**Step 6: Flesh Out the Scenarios**
Return to the key forces (Steps 2-3) and weave them into narrative scenarios built on the logics from Step 5. Each scenario should be:
- Internally consistent
- Plausible (can it be rendered logically impossible? If not, keep it)
- Named with a memorable title
- Told as a story, not a data table

**Step 7: Implications**
Return to the focal decision (Step 1). For each scenario, ask:
- What does the decision look like in this world?
- What vulnerabilities are exposed?
- What opportunities emerge?
- Is our current strategy robust across scenarios or fragile?

**Step 8: Leading Indicators and Signposts**
Identify early-warning signals that indicate which scenario is unfolding. Monitor these continuously. "It is important to know as soon as possible which of several scenarios is closest to the course of history as it actually unfolds."

## Validation Context

**Shell's 1973 oil crisis anticipation:** Shell's scenario team (under Wack) had developed scenarios including an oil price shock before OPEC's 1973 embargo. While other oil companies were caught off-guard, Shell's managers had already "rehearsed" the possibility and responded faster. Shell moved from the weakest of the "Seven Sisters" to one of the two strongest within a few years.

**Shell's continued practice:** Shell has maintained a scenario planning practice continuously since 1971 — the longest-running corporate scenario program. They publish global scenarios every few years (most recently "Scenarios: An Explorer's Guide").

**South Africa's Mont Fleur scenarios (1991-92):** GBN methodology applied to post-apartheid South Africa's transition, widely credited with helping diverse stakeholders develop shared mental models.

## Known Limitations

**From Wack himself:**
- First-generation scenarios (simple uncertainty matrices) are nearly useless — "water on a stone." The difficult part is the second generation: transforming mental models
- Scenarios must be plausible, not probable — but plausibility is subjective

**Methodological criticisms:**
- **Cognitive biases persist**: confirmation bias leads teams to create scenarios that validate existing strategies; anchoring bias causes excessive focus on current conditions
- **Groupthink**: scenario workshops can produce consensus scenarios that lack genuine diversity and fail to explore uncomfortable possibilities
- **Facilitator dependence**: quality depends heavily on the facilitator's skill and the team's willingness to challenge assumptions
- **No probabilities**: scenarios deliberately avoid assigning probabilities — but decision-makers implicitly do so anyway, often anchoring on the "most likely" scenario and ignoring others
- **Resource-intensive**: done properly, the process requires significant research, facilitation time, and senior management attention
- **Implementation gap**: even well-constructed scenarios often fail to change behavior — Wack's own warning about "water on a stone" remains the central challenge
