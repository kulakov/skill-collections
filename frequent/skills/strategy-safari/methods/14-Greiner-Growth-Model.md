# Greiner's Growth Model

## 1. Original Source

- **Author:** Larry E. Greiner (Professor, USC Marshall School of Business)
- **Article:** "Evolution and Revolution as Organizations Grow", Harvard Business Review, July-August 1972, pp. 37-46
- **Updated:** Republished in HBR, May-June 1998, with a sixth phase added and a commentary
- **Also reprinted:** Family Business Review, Vol. 10, No. 4, 1997, pp. 397-409

---

## 2. Canonical Protocol

### Core Concept: Evolution and Revolution

Greiner's key insight: organizational growth is **not smooth and continuous**. It proceeds through alternating periods of:

- **Evolution:** Extended periods of steady growth with no major upheaval. During evolution, only modest adjustments are needed to maintain growth.
- **Revolution:** Short, turbulent periods of significant organizational upheaval. During revolution, serious management problems emerge that cannot be solved by the methods that worked during the preceding evolutionary period.

**The critical point:** Management practices that work during one evolutionary period eventually **cause** the revolutionary crisis that ends that period. Success breeds the conditions for failure.

### Five Key Dimensions (Greiner's Analytical Framework)

Greiner specified five dimensions that define each phase:

1. **Age of the organization** — management practices are rooted in time; what works in year 2 won't work in year 20
2. **Size of the organization** — problems and solutions change with headcount and complexity
3. **Stage of evolution** — the prolonged period of growth with modest adjustments
4. **Stage of revolution** — the period of substantial turmoil
5. **Growth rate of the industry** — faster-growing industries compress the phases; slower industries stretch them

### The Six Phases

Each phase has: an **evolutionary management style** that drives growth, and a **revolutionary crisis** that terminates the phase.

---

#### Phase 1: Growth Through CREATIVITY

**Evolutionary period:**
- Organization is young, small
- Founders are technically or entrepreneurially oriented
- Communication is frequent and informal
- Management focus: **make and sell** the product
- Long hours, modest salaries, promise of ownership/equity
- Management reactions to customer feedback drive key decisions
- Structure: nonexistent or minimal

**Management focus:** Product creation and market entry
**Control system:** Market results (are customers buying?)
**Management style:** Individualistic, entrepreneurial
**Organizational structure:** Informal, flat

**Revolutionary crisis: CRISIS OF LEADERSHIP**
- As the company grows, production requires knowledge about manufacturing efficiencies
- Increased number of employees cannot be managed exclusively through informal communication
- Founders often hate managing; they want to create
- **The crisis:** Who is going to lead the organization out of confusion and solve the managerial problems? Usually requires bringing in a professional business manager.

---

#### Phase 2: Growth Through DIRECTION

**Evolutionary period:**
- Professional management installed
- Functional organizational structure introduced
- Accounting, inventory, purchasing, manufacturing functions formalized
- Budgets and work standards established
- Incentives, budgets, work standards adopted
- Communication becomes more formal and hierarchical
- Lower-level managers treated as functional specialists, not autonomous decision-makers

**Management focus:** Efficiency of operations
**Control system:** Standards and cost centers
**Management style:** Directive
**Organizational structure:** Centralized, functional

**Revolutionary crisis: CRISIS OF AUTONOMY**
- Centralized direction works for the growing organization, but lower-level managers increasingly feel constrained
- They possess more direct knowledge of markets and operations than top management
- They are torn between following procedures and taking initiative
- **The crisis:** Demands for greater autonomy. Top management is reluctant to give up control. The organization needs to delegate but doesn't know how.

---

#### Phase 3: Growth Through DELEGATION

**Evolutionary period:**
- Decentralized organizational structure
- Plant managers, market managers, and product managers given responsibility and autonomy
- Profit centers and bonuses used for motivation
- Top management manages by exception (periodic reports)
- Communication from the top is infrequent
- New acquisitions may be grafted onto the structure as semi-autonomous units

**Management focus:** Market expansion
**Control system:** Reports and profit centers
**Management style:** Delegative
**Organizational structure:** Decentralized, divisional

**Revolutionary crisis: CRISIS OF CONTROL**
- Autonomous field managers run their own operations without coordinating with each other or headquarters
- Parochial attitudes develop ("my division first")
- Top management regains control through special coordination techniques
- **The crisis:** Freedom has bred fragmentation. Headquarters feels it is losing control. The solution is not recentralization (that would destroy the gains of delegation) but new coordination mechanisms.

---

#### Phase 4: Growth Through COORDINATION

**Evolutionary period:**
- Decentralized units are merged into product groups
- Formal planning procedures established and intensively reviewed
- Headquarters staff expanded: planning, finance, control functions
- Capital allocation is carefully administered by headquarters
- Each unit gets budgets; ROI accountability per product group
- Technical functions (data processing, etc.) are centralized at headquarters
- Stock options and profit sharing used to align divisions with corporate goals

**Management focus:** Consolidation of the organization
**Control system:** Plans and investment centers
**Management style:** Watchdog
**Organizational structure:** Line-staff / product groups

**Revolutionary crisis: CRISIS OF RED TAPE**
- The proliferation of systems, programs, and staff functions creates bureaucratic overhead
- Line vs. staff tensions increase
- Procedures take precedence over problem-solving
- Innovation is stifled by compliance requirements
- **The crisis:** The organization is too big and complex for the formal programs and rigid systems to manage. A new, more spontaneous approach is needed.

---

#### Phase 5: Growth Through COLLABORATION

**Evolutionary period:**
- Emphasis on interpersonal collaboration and behavioral approaches
- Teams are combined across functions for task-group activity
- Matrix-type structure
- Headquarters staff reduced; previous staff experts reassigned to interdisciplinary teams as consultants (not managers)
- Educational programs for managers in team behavior and conflict resolution
- Real-time information systems integrated into daily decision-making
- Economic rewards geared more to team performance than individual achievement
- Experimentation with new practices is encouraged

**Management focus:** Problem-solving and innovation
**Control system:** Mutual goal-setting
**Management style:** Participative
**Organizational structure:** Matrix, team-based

**Revolutionary crisis: CRISIS OF INTERNAL GROWTH (or "psychological saturation")**
- Employees grow exhausted from the emotional and physical demands of teamwork and innovation
- Burnout from constant pressure to be creative
- **The crisis:** Employees and managers become saturated with the intensity of collaboration.

---

#### Phase 6: Growth Through ALLIANCES (1998 addition)

**Evolutionary period:**
- Organization recognizes it cannot solve all problems internally
- Growth through mergers, acquisitions, outsourcing, networks, and partnerships
- Organizational boundaries become more permeable
- External relationships become as strategically important as internal management

**Management focus:** Extra-organizational solutions
**Control system:** Partnership agreements, networked governance
**Management style:** Networked

**Revolutionary crisis: CRISIS OF IDENTITY**
- With boundaries blurred by alliances and acquisitions, the organization must refocus on its core identity: vision, mission, and strategy
- "Who are we?" becomes the central question
- **The crisis:** Loss of organizational coherence and identity

### How to Use for Diagnosis and Prediction

Greiner prescribed the model as a **diagnostic and predictive** tool:

1. **Diagnose current phase:** Identify which evolutionary management style currently dominates your organization (creativity? direction? delegation? coordination? collaboration?)

2. **Predict the next crisis:** Each phase contains the seeds of its own crisis. If you're in Phase 2 (direction), expect the crisis of autonomy. If you're in Phase 4 (coordination), expect the crisis of red tape.

3. **Prepare the next evolution:** The solution to each crisis is the management approach of the next phase. Knowing this in advance allows management to prepare the transition rather than being surprised by it.

4. **Avoid regression:** A common mistake is to respond to a crisis by reverting to the previous phase's management style. This always fails — you cannot solve the crisis of autonomy by returning to informal creativity.

**Greiner's specific warning:** "A management with a sense of its own organization's history can anticipate and prepare for the next developmental crisis. That knowledge should reduce trauma."

## 3. Validation Context

- **Based on:** Greiner's own consulting and research experience with organizations at various stages. The model synthesized existing organizational development literature with empirical observation.
- **Not a formal empirical study:** Unlike Quinn's work (which studied 10 specific firms), Greiner's model is a **theoretical synthesis** illustrated with examples, not a research finding from systematic data collection.
- **Widely adopted:** One of the most frequently cited models in organizational development and entrepreneurship education
- **Updated in 1998:** The HBR republication added Phase 6 and Greiner reflected on 26 years of practitioner feedback confirming the model's general pattern
- **Cross-validated informally:** Greiner noted that the model resonated strongly with managers who recognized their own organizations in specific phases

---

## 4. Known Limitations

### Empirical challenges

1. **No rigorous empirical validation:** Research has not found strong empirical evidence for the model's specific sequence. There are no general patterns confirmed in the sequence of frame-breaking changes and growth stages.

2. **Linear determinism:** The model describes growth as unavoidable and sequential. In reality, organizations may skip phases, repeat phases, or experience multiple crises simultaneously.

3. **Not all crises are inevitable:** Not every business suffers crises as it grows. Many adapt smoothly without the distinct revolutionary periods Greiner describes.

4. **Growth ≠ the only path:** The model assumes continuous growth. Organizations that stabilize, shrink, or pivot are not well-served by the framework.

5. **Industry-blind:** The model provides no adjustment for industry type, competitive dynamics, or regulatory environment. A tech startup and a manufacturing firm may experience very different growth patterns.

6. **Monocausal crisis model:** Each phase is terminated by exactly one type of crisis, implying a deterministic relationship between growth and organizational problems. Reality is more complex — multiple crises can converge.

7. **Historical context:** The model was developed during the era of large, vertically integrated corporations. Modern organizations (platform companies, remote-first firms, ecosystem businesses) may follow different growth patterns.

### Greiner's own acknowledgment (1998)

In the HBR update, Greiner acknowledged that:
- Phase transitions are not as clean as the model suggests
- Some organizations may experience phases in different sequences
- The model works best as a **thinking tool** for anticipating challenges, not as a rigid prescription
