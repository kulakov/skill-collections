# SWOT Analysis

## Original Source

**Authors:** Edmund P. Learned, C. Roland Christensen, Kenneth R. Andrews, William D. Guth (collectively: LCAG)
**Primary work:** *Business Policy: Text and Cases* (1965), Harvard Business School
**Elaboration:** Kenneth R. Andrews, *The Concept of Corporate Strategy* (1971), Irwin, Homewood IL
**Revised editions:** Christensen et al. 4th edition (1978) -- first time the LCAG framework appears as a visual scheme

### Critical Note on Attribution

The common attribution of SWOT to Andrews/Harvard is contested. In a 2017 interview, Professor William D. Guth (last surviving LCAG author) denied that SWOT analysis originated at HBS: "I am very confident that neither Ken Andrews nor any member of the business policy teaching faculty during that period created the 2x2 matrix that became so widely known and widely used in managerial application as SWOT analysis."

A parallel origin exists: Stanford Research Institute (SRI) published the **SOFT approach** (Satisfactory, Opportunity, Fault, Threat) in 1965 through Albert Humphrey's Long Range Planning Service. The SOFT approach is now considered by historians (Puyt et al., 2023) to be the direct progenitor of SWOT.

The 1965 LCAG text uses the separate words "strengths," "weaknesses," "opportunities," and "risks" -- but never the acronym SWOT and never the 2x2 matrix.

## Canonical Protocol (Andrews, 1971)

Andrews did NOT prescribe a 2x2 matrix. He prescribed a **strategy formulation process** with four components:

### The Four Components of Strategy Determination

1. **Appraisal of present and foreseeable opportunity and risk** in the company's environment (Environmental Analysis)
2. **Assessment of the firm's unique combination of present and potential corporate resources or competence** (Organizational Analysis)
3. **Determination of the noneconomic personal and organizational preferences** to be satisfied (Values & Aspirations)
4. **Identification and acceptance of the social responsibilities** of the firm (Ethical Obligations)

### The Matching Process

Strategy = the match between:
- **What the firm COULD DO** (environmental opportunity/risk) and
- **What the firm CAN DO** (organizational strengths/weaknesses)

...filtered through:
- **What the firm WANTS TO DO** (management values and aspirations)
- **What the firm SHOULD DO** (societal obligations)

### Environmental Appraisal (External)

Andrews prescribed examining:
- Economic, technological, social, and political developments
- Industry structure and competitive dynamics
- Identification of **opportunities** (favorable conditions for action)
- Identification of **risks** (unfavorable conditions; note: Andrews used "risks," not "threats")

### Corporate Appraisal (Internal)

Andrews prescribed identifying:
- Financial, managerial, functional, and organizational capabilities
- **Strengths** = distinctive competences the firm possesses
- **Weaknesses** = limitations or deficiencies relative to competitors

### The Decision

The strategist's task: select among strategic alternatives the one that best matches external opportunity with internal capability, consistent with values and responsibilities.

## The Modern 2x2 Matrix (Post-Andrews)

The familiar matrix emerged through teaching practice, not from any single canonical source:

|              | Helpful          | Harmful          |
|--------------|------------------|------------------|
| **Internal** | Strengths        | Weaknesses       |
| **External** | Opportunities    | Threats          |

Heinz Weihrich (1982) formalized the **TOWS Matrix** -- a variation that generates strategy combinations:
- **SO strategies** (use strengths to exploit opportunities)
- **WO strategies** (overcome weaknesses by exploiting opportunities)
- **ST strategies** (use strengths to avoid threats)
- **WT strategies** (minimize weaknesses and avoid threats)

## Validation Context

- Developed and tested in the **Harvard Business School case method** tradition (1960s-1970s)
- Applied across hundreds of HBS case studies spanning manufacturing, services, and diversified corporations
- Andrews' own case work focused on **General Mills, Hershey, and various diversified US corporations**
- The framework became the dominant strategy teaching tool by the 1970s and remains the most widely used strategic planning method in business education globally
- No formal empirical validation study exists -- it was validated through pedagogical practice and widespread adoption

## Known Limitations

### From Andrews' Own School

- Andrews acknowledged the difficulty of **objective self-assessment** -- firms systematically overestimate strengths and underestimate weaknesses
- The "matching" process requires **judgment that cannot be formalized** -- Andrews was explicit that strategy formulation is an art, not a science

### From Critics

1. **No prioritization or weighting:** All items in each quadrant appear equal. Ten threats are listed with no guidance on which are critical and which are marginal (Hill & Westbrook, 1997)

2. **Static snapshot:** Captures a moment in time but provides no mechanism for tracking changes. In dynamic markets, the analysis becomes outdated rapidly

3. **Subjective and biased:** Identified factors reflect the biases and blind spots of whoever conducts the analysis. Different teams analyzing the same firm produce different SWOTs

4. **No action linkage:** The basic SWOT generates lists but provides no protocol for converting them into strategy. The Weihrich TOWS matrix partially addresses this

5. **Excessive formalization critique (Mintzberg, 1994):** In *The Rise and Fall of Strategic Planning*, Mintzberg argued SWOT exemplifies the false premise that strategy can be formulaically derived from structured analysis. He considered it the main cause of excessive formalization of strategy-making

6. **Category ambiguity:** The same factor can be classified as either a strength or weakness, or as an opportunity or threat, depending on perspective (e.g., a new technology is an opportunity if you adopt it, a threat if competitors do)

7. **Internal/external boundary blur:** In practice, many factors (e.g., partnerships, ecosystem relationships, regulatory capture) resist clean categorization as internal or external
